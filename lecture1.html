Syllabus

  1. Part one: 8 weekly classes:
    • Introduction to political systems.
    • Introduction to political science: How to study politics.
    • Theories of political science (mostly from assigned readings)
    • Application of theories to relevant cases (mostly in class)
  2. Part two: Self-guided study
    • Students prepare case studies.
  3. Part three: one-day seminar:
    • Student presentations of their case studies.
    • Discussion and feedback.
    • Preparing for exam paper.

Assessment

Two parts:

  • Presentation and facilitation of discussion (50%)
    • Present a case study of your own choice.
    • Apply theory from classes in first part of module.
    • Lead a short discussion.
  • Research Paper (50%)
    • Develop a more complete understanding of specific topic investigated during the presentation.
    • 8-10 pages (excl. figures, tables, and references)
    • Concise and workable research question.
    • Independent literature review.
    • Critical discussion of your topic (must be analytical and not just descriptive!)

Student presentations (50%)

You will be marked on both your presentation and subsequent facilitation of a discussion.

You can use both of these elements to illustrate that you are able to:

  • Accurately and concisely summarise relevant parts of a case.
  • Apply the theory from the module to real-life cases.
  • Think critically about the texts/theories/topics.

Practicals:

  • 15min: 10min presentation + discussion
  • Prepare at least a couple of questions for discussion.
  • All presentations take place on 21st July.
  • It is mandatory to (actively) attend all presentations to pass this course.

Research paper (50%)

Can take many forms, but should include the following elements:

  • Title page: Spend a bit of time to develop a precise and meaningful title!
  • Introduction (1-2 pages): Present a precise research question that your paper will answer. And justify why it is relevant.
  • Main part (5-6 pages): literature review, (selected methods), theoretical framework, empirical evidence, evalutaion and analysis of data/evidence.
  • Conclusion (1-2 pages): Clear answer to your research question based on the main part.
  • References
  • Crucial that you demonstrate an ability to apply theories from class - so start early to think about potentially cases for your paper!

Hand-in deadline: 15th September 2023 through Moodle.

Limitations

Main focus on (western) democracies

  • Because we are in Germany/Europe.
  • Because political science originates and still mainly focus on democratic politics.
  • (And most countries at least claim to be democratic to some extend today)

What is a democracy?

Big question with no easy answer.

However, the word simply means rule (kratos) by the people (demos)

But many ways to define the people, and define how they should rule.

Institutions

What is an institution?

"An institution is an organization with a set of rules and practices that inform its members about their relationships with one another and how they should interact. Institutions may be formal, with written rules, or they may be informal." (Rom, Hidaka, and Walker 2022, p. 28)

Examples? Schools, families, religions, criminal gangs, etc.

Political institutions: Government agencies, local councils, parliaments, political parties, political movements, NGO's, media, international org. etc.)
