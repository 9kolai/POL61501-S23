Political Insitutions and Institutionalism

New Institutionalism(s)

Developed in 1980/90's in response to behavioural perspectives that dominated the 1960/70's.

Peter Hall and Rosemary Taylor sums up development and identify three different areas of research that is concerned with institutions.

Each of these have distinct answers to the following questions:

  • What are institutions?
  • How does institutions relate to (individual) behaviour?
  • How and where do institutions origin?
  • How do institutions change and/or persist?

Historical Institutionalism

Origins

  • Conflict between groups for scarce resources at the heart of politics.
  • Influenced by structural functionalists: See polity as an overall system of interacting parts.
  • Comparative studies of public policies in different nations, focusing on impact of national political institutions.

Definition of institutions

"By and large, they define them as the formal or informal procedures, routines, norms and conventions embedded in the organizational structure of the polity or political economy." (p. 938)

  • Focus on formal organisations, rules, and conventions.
  • Focus on assymmetrical power created by institutions.
  • Strong focus on path dependency and unintended consequences.
  • Often combine the influence of institutions with other explanaroty factors.

Relationship between institutions and individual behaviour.

Historical institutionalists embrace several answers to this question.

Two overall approaches:

  • Calculus appraoch:
    • Instrumental and strategic calculation.
    • Institutions alter expectations that an actor has about the actions others are likely to take.
    • Thus institutions can provide more or less certainty about the present and futre.
  • Cultural approach:
    • Individuals are deeply embedded in a world composed of symbols, scripts and routines.
    • These provide filters for interpretation of the world.
    • And this again affects how actors behave.

Rational Choice Institutionalism

Origins

Rational-choice refers to theories that assume humans are rational beings. (very simplified)

  • Inspired by puzzle about collective actions problems.
  • Institutions lower the transaction-costs of makings deals, to allow outcomes that would otherwise be irrational.

Four characteristics:

  • Behavioural assumptions about fixed preferences.
  • See politics as a series of collective action dilemmas.
  • Emphasize the role of strategic interaction.
  • Institutions exists as voluntary agreements by individual actors.
  • The last point leads to a notion that insitutions only survive if they provide enough benefits to the relevant actors.

Sociological Institutionalism

Origins

Comes from organistional theory in sociology.

Reaction against earlier notion that organisations evolved to become more efficient over time.

More broad definition of institutions:

  • No distinction between organisational "institutional" explenations and cultural explenations.
  • Redifinition of "culture" as a network of symbols, routines, and scripts rather than individual values or attitudes.

Extreme focus on cultural approach:

Institutions define...

  • ..what is acceptable.
  • ...but also what is even possible.

...which leads to a mutually-constitutive relationship between insitutions and individual action.

Persistance of institutions

A "logic of social appropiateness" instead of a "logic of appropriateness".

What is "legitimate" and "socially appropriate"?

A matter of cultural authority.

Some examples of neo-institutionalism.

Martin Lodge: Reforming UK and GE Railway Regulation

Privatisation and vertical integration/separation of railways

Roughly speaking you need two things to run railways:

A railway network.

Trains.

Vertical integration:

One organisation

A railway network.

Trains.

Vertical Separation:

Separate organisations

A railway network.

Trains.

Puzzle to explain:

Why did railway reform in UK and Germany turn out so differently?

Ie. which institutional differences between UK and Germany might explain the different policy outcomes?

Great Britain

  • All parts of railway system privatised completely (ie. no public ownership)
  • Railway infrastructure -> one independeent private company.
  • Train services split into many smaller private companies (with franchising by public authorities)

Germany

  • Vertical separation within new DB holding company (Infrastructure and train services in separate companies, but with shared owner).
  • DB holding company turned into private law company, but kept under public ownership.
  • Responsibility for local and regional rail delegated to LÃ¤nder.

Theoretical framework:

Some possible institutional factors explaining differences:

  • Coercive pressures: Pressure by other organisations, which an organisation is dependent on.
  • Org. of political-administrative nexus:
    • Administrative capacities <= different types of "on-the-job learning" (Dienstwissen) <= typical career-paths of civil servants. ("lateral transfer vs. "pantouflage" vs. domain-specific expertise)
    • Dominant overarching deparments
    • Length of ministerial appointments (=> strength of ministers vs. bureaucracy)
  • Access of societal actors: Different institutional setups allow diffferent interest groups access to the decision-making process.

Findings and conclusions

  • Coercive pressures: Played little role in both cases.
    • EU regulation existed, but both cases went beyond requirements, and mainly used that to justify own positions.
    • Experiences from other countries were either not seeked at all or rejected routinely.
  • Political-administrative nexus: Strong evidence of crucial impact.
    • "Lateral transfer" of public servants with privatisation experience in UK vs. civil servants and experts from the railway domain in Ge.
    • UK Treasury and weak UK government vs. many political veto points in Germany lead to more room for civil servants in UK to dominate.
  • Access of societal actors: Limited role, but some differences between cases:
    • UK: BR cut off from process and potential future franchisees not included.
    • Germany: DB, academics and unions directly involved in reform commission... but according to author only because they served the politically suported positions.

Burkard Eberlein: German Electricity Reform

Puzzle to explain:

  • Liberalisation of German infrastructure,
  • which was previously very state dominated (either state-owned, state-run, or state licensed monopoly)
  • Substantial change of behaviour among market actors.
  • But strong continuity of regulatory patterns.
  • Want to explain the actual scope and effects of market reforms in Germany.
  • Exemplified with the German electricity reform.
